<div lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="dablink">For the R.E.M. album, see <a href="/wiki/Document_(album)" title="Document (album)">Document (album)</a>.</div>

<div class="dablink">For the similarly named surrealist journal, see <a href="/wiki/Documents_(magazine)" title="Documents (magazine)">Documents (magazine)</a>.</div>

<p><br />

The term <b>document</b> has more meanings in ordinary language and in scholarship. <a href="/wiki/WordNet" title="WordNet">WordNet</a> 3.1. lists four meanings (October 2011):</p>

<ul>

<li>document, written document, papers (writing that provides information (especially information of an official nature))</li>

<li>document (anything serving as a representation of a person's thinking by means of symbolic marks)</li>

<li>document (a written account of ownership or obligation)</li>

<li>text file, document ((computer science) a computer file that contains text (and possibly formatting instructions) using seven-bit ASCII characters).</li>

</ul>

<p><br />

In <a href="/wiki/Library_and_information_science" title="Library and information science">Library and information science</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Documentation_science" title="Documentation science">documentation science</a> is "document" considered a basic theoretical construct. It is everything which may be preserved or represented in order to serve as evidence for some purpose. The classical example provided by <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Briet" title="Suzanne Briet">Suzanne Briet</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelope</a>: "An antelope running wild on the plains of Africa should not be considered a document, she rules. But if it were to be captured, taken to a zoo and made an object of study, it has been made into a document. It has become physical evidence being used by those who study it. Indeed, scholarly articles written about the antelope are secondary documents, since the antelope itself is the primary document." (Quoted from Buckland, 1998 <sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>). (This view has been seen as an early expression of what now is known as <a href="/wiki/Actor%E2%80%93network_theory" title="Actor–network theory">actor–network theory</a>).</p>

<p>That documents cannot be defined by their <a href="/wiki/Transmission_medium" title="Transmission medium">transmission medium</a> (such as paper) is evident because of the existence of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_document" title="Electronic document">electronic documents</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: What is a document?">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="What_is_a_document.3F">What is a document?</span></h2>

<p>The concept of document has been defined as <b>“any concrete or symbolic indication, preserved or recorded, for reconstructing or for proving a phenomenon, whether physical or mental"</b> (Briet, 1951, 7; here quoted from Buckland, 1991).</p>

<p>A much cited article asked "what is a document" and concluded this way: “The evolving notion of ‘‘document’’ among <a href="/wiki/Paul_Otlet" title="Paul Otlet">Otlet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Briet" title="Suzanne Briet">Briet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Sch%C3%BCrmeyer" title="Walter Schürmeyer">Schürmeyer</a>, and the other <a href="/wiki/Documentalist" title="Documentalist">documentalists</a> increasingly emphasized whatever functioned as a document rather than traditional physical forms of documents. The shift to digital technology would seem to make this distinction even more important. <a href="/wiki/David_M_Levy" title="David M Levy">Levy</a>’s thoughtful analyses have shown that an emphasis on the technology of digital documents has impeded our understanding of digital documents as documents (e.g., Levy, 1994<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup>) . A conventional document, such as a mail message or a technical report, exists physically in digital technology as a string of bits, but so does everything else in a digital environment. In this sense, any distinctiveness of a document as a physical form is further diminished, and discussion of ‘‘What is a digital document?’’ becomes even more problematic unless we remember the path of reasoning underlying the largely forgotten discussions of Otlet’s objects and Briet’s antelope.” (Buckland, 1997, p. 808<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup>).</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of documents">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_documents">Types of documents</span></h2>

<p>Documents are sometimes classified as <a href="/wiki/Classified_information" title="Classified information">secret</a>, <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">private</a> or public. They may also be described as a <a href="/wiki/Draft_document" title="Draft document">draft</a> or <a href="/wiki/Proofreading" title="Proofreading">proof</a>. When a document is <a href="/wiki/Copying" title="Copying">copied</a>, the source is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Originality" title="Originality">original</a>.</p>

<p>There are accepted standards for specific applications in various fields, such as:</p>

<ul>

<li>a7a: <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">thesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dissertation" title="Dissertation" class="mw-redirect">dissertation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journal" title="Journal">journal</a></li>

<li>Business and accounting: <a href="/wiki/Invoice" title="Invoice">Invoice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Financial_quote" title="Financial quote">quote</a>, <a href="/wiki/RFP" title="RFP" class="mw-redirect">RFP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proposal" title="Proposal">Proposal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">Contract</a>, <a href="/wiki/Packing_slip" title="Packing slip" class="mw-redirect">Packing slip</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manifest" title="Manifest">Manifest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Report" title="Report">Report</a> detailed &amp; summary, <a href="/wiki/Spread_sheet" title="Spread sheet" class="mw-redirect">Spread sheet</a>, <a href="/wiki/MSDS" title="MSDS" class="mw-redirect">MSDS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waybill" title="Waybill">Waybill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_Lading" title="Bill of Lading" class="mw-redirect">Bill of Lading</a> (BOL), <a href="/wiki/Statement" title="Statement">Statement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nondisclosure_agreement" title="Nondisclosure agreement" class="mw-redirect">Nondisclosure agreement</a> (NDA) or sometimes referred to as; Mutual nondisclosure agreement (MNDA)</li>

<li>Law and politics: <a href="/wiki/Summons" title="Summons">summons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Certificate" title="Certificate">certificate</a>, <a href="/wiki/License" title="License">license</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gazette" title="Gazette">gazette</a></li>

<li>Government and industry: <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">white paper</a></li>

<li>Media and marketing: <a href="/wiki/Brief" title="Brief">brief</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mock-up" title="Mock-up" class="mw-redirect">mock-up</a>, <a href="/wiki/Script" title="Script">script</a></li>

</ul>

<p>Such standard documents can be created based on a <a href="/wiki/Template_(word_processing)" title="Template (word processing)">template</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Developing documents">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Developing_documents">Developing documents</span></h2>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Page_layout" title="Page layout">page layout</a> of a document is the manner in which information is graphically arranged in the document space (e.g., on a page). If the appearance of the document is of concern, page layout is generally the responsibility of a <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic designer</a>. <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">Typography</a> deals with the design of letter and symbol forms, as well as their physical arrangement in the document (see <a href="/wiki/Typesetting" title="Typesetting">typesetting</a>). <a href="/wiki/Information_design" title="Information design">Information design</a> focuses on the effective communication of <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, especially in industrial documents and public <a href="/wiki/Sign" title="Sign">signs</a>. Simple text documents may not require a visual design and may be handled by an <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data_entry_clerk" title="Data entry clerk">clerk</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transcriber" title="Transcriber">transcriber</a>. <a href="/wiki/Form_(document)" title="Form (document)">Forms</a> may require a visual design for the initial fields, but not to fill out the forms.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>

<p>Traditionally, the <a href="/wiki/Material" title="Material">medium</a> of a document was <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> and the information was applied to it as <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">ink</a>, either by hand (to make a hand-written document) or by a mechanical process (such as a <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> or, more recently, a <a href="/wiki/Laser_printer" title="Laser printer">laser printer</a>).</p>

<p>Through time, documents have also been written with ink on <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> (starting in ancient <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a>; scratched as <a href="/wiki/Runes" title="Runes" class="mw-redirect">runes</a> on stone using a sharp apparatus; stamped or cut into <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> and then baked to make <a href="/wiki/Clay_tablet" title="Clay tablet">clay tablets</a> (e.g., in the <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamian</a> civilisations). The paper, papyrus or parchment might be rolled up as a <a href="/wiki/Scroll_(parchment)" title="Scroll (parchment)" class="mw-redirect">scroll</a> or cut into sheets and <a href="/wiki/Bookbinding" title="Bookbinding">bound</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Book" title="Book">book</a>. Today short documents might also consist of sheets of paper <a href="/wiki/Staple_(fastener)" title="Staple (fastener)">stapled</a> together.</p>

<p>Modern electronic means of storing and displaying documents include:</p>

<ul>

<li><a href="/wiki/Desktop_computer" title="Desktop computer">desktop computer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_display" title="Computer display" class="mw-redirect">monitor</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">laptop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tablet_PC" title="Tablet PC" class="mw-redirect">tablet PC</a>, etc.); optionally with a <a href="/wiki/Computer_printer" title="Computer printer" class="mw-redirect">printer</a> to obtain a <a href="/wiki/Hard_copy" title="Hard copy">hard copy</a></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">Personal digital assistant</a> (PDA)</li>

<li>dedicated <a href="/wiki/E-book_device" title="E-book device" class="mw-redirect">e-book device</a></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Electronic_paper" title="Electronic paper">electronic paper</a></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Information_appliance" title="Information appliance">information appliances</a></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Digital_audio_player" title="Digital audio player" class="mw-redirect">digital audio players</a></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Service_Provider" title="Telecommunications Service Provider" class="mw-redirect">service provider</a></li>

</ul>

<p>Digital documents usually have to adhere to a specific <a href="/wiki/File_format" title="File format">file format</a> in order to be useful.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Document&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In law">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="In_law">In law</span></h2>

<p>Documents in all forms are frequently found to be material <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> in criminal and civil proceedings. The <a href="/wiki/Forensic" title="Forensic" class="mw-redirect">forensic</a> analysis of such a document falls under the scope of <a href="/wiki/Questioned_document_examination" title="Questioned document examination">questioned document examination</a>. For the purpose of cataloging and managing the large number of documents that may be produced in the course of a law suit, <a href="/wiki/Bates_numbering" title="Bates numbering">Bates numbering</a> is often applied to all documents so that each document has a unique, aribitrary identifying number.</p>
