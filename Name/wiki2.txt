<p>A <b>street</b> is a paved <a href="/wiki/Public" title="Public">public</a> <a href="/wiki/Thoroughfare" title="Thoroughfare">thoroughfare</a> in a built environment. It is a <a href="/wiki/Public" title="Public">public</a> parcel of <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">land</a> adjoining <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">buildings</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> context, on which people may freely assemble, interact, and move about. A street can be as simple as a level patch of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">dirt</a>, but is more often <a href="/wiki/Pavement_(material)" title="Pavement (material)" class="mw-redirect">paved</a> with a hard, durable surface such as <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobblestone" title="Cobblestone">cobblestone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a>. Portions may also be smoothed with <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a>, embedded with <a href="/wiki/Rail_tracks" title="Rail tracks" class="mw-redirect">rails</a>, or otherwise prepared to accommodate non-<a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">pedestrian</a> traffic.</p>

<p>Originally the word "street" simply meant a paved road (Latin: "via strata"). The word "street" is still sometimes used colloquially as a synonym for "<a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">road</a>", for example in connection with the ancient <a href="/wiki/Watling_Street" title="Watling Street">Watling Street</a>, but city residents and <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planners</a> draw a crucial modern distinction: a road's main function is transportation, while streets facilitate public interaction.<sup id="cite_ref-Dictionary_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Yahoo.21_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo.21-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Examples of streets include <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_street" title="Pedestrian street" class="mw-redirect">pedestrian streets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alley" title="Alley">alleys</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Downtown" title="Downtown">city-centre</a> streets too crowded for <a href="/wiki/Road_vehicle" title="Road vehicle" class="mw-redirect">road vehicles</a> to pass. Conversely, <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highways</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motorway" title="Motorway" class="mw-redirect">motorways</a> are types of roads, but few would refer to them as streets.<sup id="cite_ref-Using_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Using-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Avenue_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avenue-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>

The Porta Rosa was the main street of <a href="/wiki/Velia" title="Velia">Elea</a>, connecting the northern quarter with the southern quarter. The street is 5 meters wide and has an incline of 18&#160;% in the steepest part. It is paved with limestone blocks, griders cut in square blocks, and on one side a small gutter for the drainage of rain water. The building is dated during the time of the reorganization of the city during the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_age" title="Hellenistic age" class="mw-redirect">Hellenistic age</a> (IV–III centuries BC).</div>

</div>

</div>

<p>The word <i>street</i> has its origins in the Latin <i>strata</i> (meaning "paved road"); it is thus related to <i><a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">stratum</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Stratification_(archeology)" title="Stratification (archeology)">stratification</a></i>. Ancient Greek <i><a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%83%CF%84%CF%81%CE%B1%CF%84%CF%8C%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:στρατός">Stratos</a></i> means army; Greeks originally built roads to move their armies. <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> applied the word to <a href="/wiki/Roman_roads_in_Britain" title="Roman roads in Britain">Roman roads in Britain</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Ermin_Street" title="Ermin Street">Ermin Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Watling_Street" title="Watling Street">Watling Street</a>, etc. Later it acquired a dialectical meaning of "straggling village", which were often laid out on the verges of Roman roads and these settlements often became named <a href="/wiki/Stretton" title="Stretton">Stretton</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, a <i>road</i> was a way people travelled, with <i>street</i> applied specifically to paved ways.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role in the built environment">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_the_built_environment">Role in the built environment</span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St.JamesSt.-Montreal_-1910.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/St.JamesSt.-Montreal_-1910.jpg/220px-St.JamesSt.-Montreal_-1910.jpg" width="220" height="325" class="thumbimage" /></a>

<div class="thumbcaption">

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St.JamesSt.-Montreal_-1910.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.18/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<a href="/wiki/Saint_Jacques_Street" title="Saint Jacques Street">Rue Saint-Jacques</a>, a street in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, 1910</div>

</div>

</div>

<p>The street is a public <a href="/wiki/Easement" title="Easement">easement</a>, one of the few shared between all sorts of people. As a component of the <a href="/wiki/Built_environment" title="Built environment">built environment</a> as <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient</a> as human habitation, the street sustains a range of activities vital to <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>. Its roles are as numerous and diverse as its ever-changing cast of characters.</p>

<p>Streets can be loosely categorized as <a href="/wiki/Main_street" title="Main street" class="mw-redirect">main streets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Side_street" title="Side street" class="mw-redirect">side streets</a>. Main streets are usually broad with a relatively high level of activity. Commerce and public interaction are more visible on main streets, and vehicles may use them for longer-distance travel. Side streets are quieter, often residential in use and character, and may be used for vehicular parking.</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Circulation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Circulation">Circulation</span></h3>

<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Traffic" title="Traffic">Traffic</a></div>

<div class="rellink boilerplate seealso">See also: <a href="/wiki/Street_network" title="Street network">Street network</a></div>

<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JalanAmpangNight.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/JalanAmpangNight.jpg/220px-JalanAmpangNight.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" /></a>

<div class="thumbcaption">

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JalanAmpangNight.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.18/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<a href="/wiki/Jalan_Ampang" title="Jalan Ampang">Jalan Ampang</a> at night, with the <a href="/wiki/Petronas_Twin_Towers" title="Petronas Twin Towers" class="mw-redirect">Petronas Twin Towers</a> visible in the background in <a href="/wiki/Kuala_Lumpur" title="Kuala Lumpur">Kuala Lumpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>.</div>

</div>

</div>

<p>Circulation, or less broadly, <a href="/wiki/Transportation" title="Transportation" class="mw-redirect">transportation</a>, is perhaps a street's most visible use, and certainly among the most important. The unrestricted movement of people and <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics_and_accounting)" title="Good (economics and accounting)" class="mw-redirect">goods</a> within a city is essential to its <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a> and vitality, and streets provide the physical space for this activity.</p>

<p>In the interest of order and efficiency, an effort may be made to segregate different types of traffic. This is usually done by carving a <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">road</a> through the middle for motorists, reserving <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">pavements</a> on either side for pedestrians; other arrangements allow for <a href="/wiki/Streetcar" title="Streetcar" class="mw-redirect">streetcars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trolley_bus" title="Trolley bus" class="mw-redirect">trolleys</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> and rainfall <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> ditches (common in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>). In the mid-20th century, as the automobile threatened to overwhelm city streets with pollution and ghastly accidents, many urban theorists came to see this segregation as not only helpful but necessary in order to maintain mobility. <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a>, for one, perceived an ever-stricter segregation of traffic as an essential affirmation of social order — a desirable, and ultimately inevitable, expression of modernity. To this end, proposals were advanced to build "vertical streets" where road vehicles, pedestrians, and trains would each occupy their own levels. Such an arrangement, it was said, would allow for even denser development in the future.</p>

<p>These plans were never implemented comprehensively, a fact which today's urban theorists regard as fortunate for vitality and diversity. Rather, vertical segregation is applied on a piecemeal basis, as in <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sewers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utility_pole" title="Utility pole">utility poles</a>, depressed highways, elevated railways, <a href="/wiki/Common_utility_duct" title="Common utility duct">common utility ducts</a>, the extensive complex of underground malls surrounding <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Station" title="Tokyo Station" class="mw-redirect">Tokyo Station</a> and the <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctemachi" title="Ōtemachi">Ōtemachi</a> subway station, the elevated pedestrian <a href="/wiki/Skyway" title="Skyway">skyway</a> networks of <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Underground_city" title="Underground city">underground cities</a> of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Multilevel_streets_in_Chicago" title="Multilevel streets in Chicago">multilevel streets in Chicago</a>.</p>

<p>Transportation is often misunderstood to be the defining characteristic, or even the sole purpose, of a street. This has not been the case since the word "street" came to be limited to urban situations, and even in the automobile age, is still demonstrably false. A street may be temporarily blocked to all through traffic in order to secure the space for other uses, such as a <a href="/wiki/Street_fair" title="Street fair">street fair</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Flea_market" title="Flea market">flea market</a>, children at play, filming a movie, or construction work. Many streets are bracketed by <a href="/wiki/Bollard" title="Bollard">bollards</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jersey_barrier" title="Jersey barrier">Jersey barriers</a> so as to keep out vehicles. These measures are often taken in a city's busiest areas, the "destination" districts, when the volume of activity outgrows the capacity of private passenger vehicles to support it. A feature universal to all streets is a human-scale design that gives its users the space and security to feel engaged in their surroundings, whatever through traffic may pass.</p>

<p>Despite this, the operator of a motor vehicle may (incompletely) regard a street as merely a thoroughfare for vehicular travel or <a href="/wiki/Parking" title="Parking">parking</a>. As far as concerns the driver, a street can be <b>one-way</b> or <b>two-way</b>: vehicles on one-way streets may travel in only one direction, while those on two-way streets may travel both ways. One way streets typically have signs reading "ONE WAY" and an arrow showing the direction of allowed travel. Most two-way streets are wide enough for at least two lanes of traffic.</p>

<p>Which lane is for which <a href="/wiki/Driving_on_the_left_or_right" title="Driving on the left or right" class="mw-redirect">direction of traffic</a> depends on what country the street is located in. On broader two-way streets, there is often a <i>center line</i> <a href="/wiki/Road_surface_marking" title="Road surface marking">marked</a> down the middle of the street separating those lanes on which vehicular traffic goes in one direction from other lanes in which traffic goes in the opposite direction. Occasionally, there may be a <a href="/wiki/Median_strip" title="Median strip" class="mw-redirect">median strip</a> separating lanes of opposing traffic. If there is more than one lane going in one direction on a main street, these lanes may be separated by intermittent <i>lane lines</i> marked on the street pavement. Side streets often do not have center lines or lane lines.</p>

<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Parking for vehicles">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Parking_for_vehicles">Parking for vehicles</span></h4>

<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parking" title="Parking">Parking</a></div>

<p>Many streets, especially <a href="/wiki/Side_street" title="Side street" class="mw-redirect">side streets</a> in residential areas, have an extra lane's width on either or both sides for <a href="/wiki/Parallel_parking" title="Parallel parking">parallel parking</a>. Most minor side streets allowing free parallel parking do not have pavement markings designating the parking lane. <a href="/wiki/Main_street" title="Main street" class="mw-redirect">Main streets</a> more often have parking lanes marked. Some streets are too busy or narrow for parking on the side. Sometimes parking on the sides of streets is allowed only at certain times. Curbside signs often state regulations about parking. Some streets, particularly in business areas, may have <a href="/wiki/Parking_meter" title="Parking meter">parking meters</a> into which coins must be paid to allow parking in the adjacent space for a limited time. Other parking meters work on a credit card and ticket basis or <a href="/wiki/Pay_and_display" title="Pay and display">pay and display</a>. Parking lane markings on the pavement may designate the meter corresponding to a parking space. Some wide streets with light traffic allow <i>angle parking</i>.</p>

<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sidewalk and bicycle traffic">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Sidewalk_and_bicycle_traffic">Sidewalk and bicycle traffic</span></h4>

<p><a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">Sidewalks</a> (US usage) or pavements (UK usage) are often located alongside on one or usually both sides of the street within the public land strips beyond the curbs. Sidewalks serve a traffic purpose, by making walking easier and more attractive, but they also serve a social function, allowing neighbors to meet and interact on their walks. They also can foster economic activity, such as window shopping and <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk_cafe" title="Sidewalk cafe">sidewalk cafes</a>. Some studies have found that shops on streets with sidewalks get more customers than similar shops without sidewalks.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>An important element of sidewalk design is accessibility for persons with disabilities. Features that make sidewalks more accessible include <a href="/w/index.php?title=Curb_ramps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Curb ramps (page does not exist)">curb ramps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tactile_paving" title="Tactile paving">tactile paving</a> and accessible traffic signals. The <a href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act" title="Americans with Disabilities Act" class="mw-redirect">Americans with Disabilities Act</a> requires accessibility improvement on new and reconstructed streets within the US.</p>

<p>In most jurisdictions, bicycles are legally allowed to use streets, and required to follow the same laws as motor vehicle traffic, except those inapplicable by their nature, such as emission inspections. Where the volume of bicycle traffic warrants and available right-of-way allows, provisions may be made to separate bicyclists from motor vehicle traffic. Wider lanes may be provided next to the curb, or shoulders may provided. <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_lanes" title="Bicycle lanes" class="mw-redirect">Bicycle lanes</a> may be used on busy streets to provide some separation between bicycle traffic and motor vehicle traffic. The bicycle lane may be placed between the travel lanes and the parking lanes, or between the parking lanes and the curb.</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vehicular amenities and roadside hardware">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicular_amenities_and_roadside_hardware">Vehicular amenities and roadside hardware</span></h3>

<p>Often, a <a href="/wiki/Curb" title="Curb">curb</a> is used to separate the vehicle traffic lanes from the adjacent sidewalk area. <a href="/wiki/Street_signs" title="Street signs" class="mw-redirect">Street signs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parking_meter" title="Parking meter">parking meters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_stand" title="Bicycle stand">bicycle stands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bench_(furniture)" title="Bench (furniture)">benches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traffic_signals" title="Traffic signals" class="mw-redirect">traffic signals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Street_light" title="Street light">street lights</a> are often found adjacent to streets. They may be behind the sidewalk, or between the sidewalk and the curb.</p>

<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Landscaping">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Landscaping">Landscaping</span></h4>

<p>There may be a <a href="/wiki/Road_verge" title="Road verge">road verge</a> (a strip of grass or other vegetation) between the <a href="/wiki/Carriageway" title="Carriageway">carriageway</a> (North American English: Roadway) and the pavement on either side of the street on which <a href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">Grass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> are often grown there for <a href="/wiki/Landscaping" title="Landscaping">landscaping</a>. These are often placed for beautification, but are increasingly being used to control <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Utilities">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Utilities">Utilities</span></h3>

<p>Although primarily used for traffic, streets are important corridors for utilities such as electric power, communications such as telephone, cable television and fiber optic lines, storm and <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewers" title="Sanitary sewers" class="mw-redirect">sanitary sewers</a>, and natural gas lines.</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Street numbering">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Street_numbering">Street numbering</span></h3>

<p>Practically all public streets in Western countries and the majority elsewhere (though not in Japan; see <a href="/wiki/Japanese_addressing_system" title="Japanese addressing system">Japanese addressing system</a>) are given a <a href="/wiki/Street_name" title="Street name" class="mw-redirect">street name</a> or at least a number to identify them and any <a href="/wiki/Address_(geography)" title="Address (geography)">addresses</a> located along the streets. <a href="/wiki/Alley" title="Alley">Alleys</a> typically do not have names. The length of a lot of land along a street is referred to as the <i>frontage</i> of the lot.</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Interaction">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction">Interaction</span></h3>

<p>A street may assume the role of a <a href="/wiki/Town_square" title="Town square">town square</a> for its regulars. <a href="/wiki/Jane_Jacobs" title="Jane Jacobs">Jane Jacobs</a>, an economist and prominent urbanist, wrote extensively on the ways that interaction among the people who live and work on a particular street—"<a href="/wiki/Mass_surveillance" title="Mass surveillance">eyes on the street</a>"—can reduce crime, encourage the exchange of ideas, and generally make the world a better place.</p>

<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Street&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Identity">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Identity">Identity</span></h3>

<p>A street can often serve as the <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst" class="mw-redirect">catalyst</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Neighborhood" title="Neighborhood" class="mw-redirect">neighborhood</a>'s prosperity, <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> and <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/solidarity" class="extiw" title="wikt:solidarity">solidarity</a>. <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>’ <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Street" title="Bourbon Street">Bourbon Street</a> is famous not only for its active <a href="/wiki/Nightlife_(activity)" title="Nightlife (activity)" class="mw-redirect">nightlife</a> but also for its role as the center of the city’s <a href="/wiki/French_Quarter" title="French Quarter">French Quarter</a>. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Bowery,_Manhattan" title="Bowery, Manhattan" class="mw-redirect">the Bowery</a> has at various times been <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>'s main highway, theater district, <a href="/wiki/Red-light_district" title="Red-light district">red-light district</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skid_row" title="Skid row">skid row</a>, restaurant supply district, and the center of the <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Underground_music" title="Underground music">underground</a> <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk</a> <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">scene</a>. <a href="/wiki/Madison_Avenue" title="Madison Avenue">Madison Avenue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Street" title="Fleet Street">Fleet Street</a> are so strongly identified with their respective most famous types of commerce, that their names are sometimes applied to firms located elsewhere. Other streets mark divisions between neighborhoods of a city. For example, <a href="/wiki/Yonge_Street" title="Yonge Street">Yonge Street</a> divides <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> into east and west sides, and <a href="/wiki/East_Capitol_Street" title="East Capitol Street">East Capitol Street</a> divides <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> into north and south.</p>

<p>Some streets are associated with the beautification of a town or city. <a href="/wiki/Greenwood,_Mississippi" title="Greenwood, Mississippi">Greenwood, Mississippi</a>'s Grand Boulevard was once named one of America's ten most beautiful streets by the U.S. Chambers of Commerce and the Garden Clubs of America. The 1000 oak trees lining Grand Boulevard were planted in 1916 by Sally Humphreys Gwin, a charter member of the Greenwood Garden Club. In 1950, Gwin received a citation from the National Congress of the <a href="/wiki/Daughters_of_the_American_Revolution" title="Daughters of the American Revolution">Daughters of the American Revolution</a> in recognition of her work in the conservation of trees.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Streets also tend to aggregate establishments of similar nature and character. <a href="/w/index.php?title=East_9th_Street_(Manhattan)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="East 9th Street (Manhattan) (page does not exist)">East 9th Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, for example, offers a cluster of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> restaurants, clothing stores, and cultural venues. In Washington, D.C., 17th Street and P Street are well-known as epicenters of the city's (relatively small) gay culture. Many cities have a <a href="/wiki/Radio_Row" title="Radio Row">Radio Row</a> or <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">Restaurant Row</a>. Like in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> there is a small street called <a href="/wiki/Jewelers%27_Row,_Philadelphia" title="Jewelers' Row, Philadelphia" class="mw-redirect">Jewelers' row</a> giving the identity of a "Diamond district". This phenomenon is the subject of urban <a href="/wiki/Location_theory" title="Location theory">location theory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>.</p>

<div class="thumb tright">

<a href="/wiki/Beirut_Central_District" title="Beirut Central District">Centre Ville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a></div>

</div>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">road</a>, like a street, is often paved and used for <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">travel</a>. However, a street is characterized by the degree and quality of street life it facilitates, whereas a road serves primarily as a through passage for <a href="/wiki/Road_vehicle" title="Road vehicle" class="mw-redirect">road vehicles</a> or (less frequently) <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">pedestrians</a>. <a href="/wiki/Busker" title="Busker" class="mw-redirect">Buskers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beggar" title="Beggar" class="mw-redirect">beggars</a>, boulevardiers, patrons of pavement <a href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9" title="Café">cafés</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peoplewatching" title="Peoplewatching" class="mw-redirect">peoplewatchers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Streetwalker" title="Streetwalker" class="mw-redirect">streetwalkers</a>, and a diversity of other characters are habitual users of a street; the same people would not typically be found on a road.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Rural" title="Rural" class="mw-redirect">rural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suburban" title="Suburban" class="mw-redirect">suburban</a> environments where street life is rare, the terms "street" and "road" are frequently considered interchangeable. Still, even here, what is called a "street" is usually a smaller thoroughfare, such as a road within a <a href="/wiki/Housing_development" title="Housing development" class="mw-redirect">housing development</a> feeding directly into individual <a href="/wiki/Driveway" title="Driveway">driveways</a>. In the last half of the 20th century these streets often abandoned the tradition of a rigid, rectangular <a href="/wiki/Grid_plan" title="Grid plan">grid</a>, and instead were designed to discourage through traffic. This and other <a href="/wiki/Traffic_calming" title="Traffic calming">traffic calming</a> methods provided quiet for families and play space for children. Adolescent suburbanites find, in attenuated form, the amenities of street life in <a href="/wiki/Shopping_mall" title="Shopping mall">shopping malls</a> where vehicles are forbidden.</p>

<p>If a road connects places, then a street connects people. One may "hit the road" to see the <a href="/wiki/Wonders_of_the_world" title="Wonders of the world" class="mw-redirect">wonders of the world</a>—<a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">Jack Kerouac</a> famously <a href="/wiki/On_the_Road" title="On the Road">chronicled</a> one such journey—but the latest <a href="/wiki/Bling" title="Bling" class="mw-redirect">bling</a> will "hit the streets" before it ever appears on a road. It is "on the street" where one hears an interesting <a href="/wiki/Rumor" title="Rumor">rumor</a>, where one bumps into an old acquaintance, where one acquires <a href="/wiki/How-to" title="How-to">street smarts</a>. One seldom sees a "road" vendor except of fresh produce, or a "road" performer. You'll never find yourself on a long "street" to nowhere or under assault by a violent "road" gang, hence politicians seldom view with alarm the prevalence of "crime in the roads". The street, not the road is home to the homeless unless they are <a href="/wiki/Hobo" title="Hobo">hoboes</a>, and even Kerouac's hero finally returned to find his friends on a New York street.</p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Town_square" title="Town square">town square</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plaza" title="Plaza">plaza</a> is a little more like a street, but a town square is rarely paved with <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a> and may not make any concessions for through traffic at all.</p>

<p>There is a haphazard relationship, at best, between a thoroughfare's function and its name. For example, London's <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road_(street)" title="Abbey Road (street)">Abbey Road</a> serves all the vital functions of a street, despite its name, and locals are more apt to refer to the "street" outside than the "road". A desolate road in rural <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, on the other hand, may bear a sign proclaiming it "Davidson Street", but this does not make it a "street" except in the original sense of a paved road.</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> many towns will refer to their main thoroughfare as the <a href="/wiki/High_Street" title="High Street">High Street</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> it would be called the <a href="/wiki/Main_Street" title="Main Street">Main Street</a> — however, occasionally "Main Street" in a city or town is a street other than the <i>de facto</i> main thoroughfare), and many of the ways leading off it will be named "Road" despite the urban setting. Thus the town's so-called "Roads" will actually be more street like than a road.</p>

<p>Some streets may even be called highways. <a href="/wiki/Hurontario_Street" title="Hurontario Street">Hurontario Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mississauga" title="Mississauga">Mississauga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada, is commonly referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Highway_10_(Ontario)" title="Highway 10 (Ontario)" class="mw-redirect">Highway 10</a>" — even though such a highway designation no longer officially exists. This is probably due to the fact that the street is a modern suburban arterial that was urbanized after decades of having the status and function a true highway, so people continued to use the number because of force of habit.</p>

<p>In some other English-speaking countries, such as New Zealand and Australia, cities are often divided by a main "Road," with "Streets" leading from this "Road", or are divided by thoroughfares known as "Streets" or "Roads" with no apparent differentiation between the two. In <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a>, for example, the main shopping precinct is around <a href="/wiki/Queen_Street,_Auckland" title="Queen Street, Auckland">Queen Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karangahape_Road" title="Karangahape Road">Karangahape Road</a>.</p>

<p>Streets have existed for as long as humans have lived in permanent settlements (see <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>). However, modern civilization in much of the New World developed around transportation provided by motor vehicles. In some parts of the English-speaking world, such as North America, many think of the street as a thoroughfare for <a href="/wiki/Vehicular_traffic" title="Vehicular traffic" class="mw-redirect">vehicular traffic</a> first and foremost. In this view, pedestrian traffic is incidental to the street's purpose; a street consists of a thoroughfare running through the middle (in essence, a <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">road</a>), and may or may not have <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">pavements</a> along the sides.</p>

<p>In an even narrower sense, some may think of a street as only the vehicle-driven and <a href="/wiki/Parking" title="Parking">parking</a> part of the thoroughfare. Thus, <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">pavements</a> and <a href="/wiki/Road_verge" title="Road verge">road verges</a> would not be thought of as part of the street. A mother may tell her toddlers "Don't go out into the street, so you don't get hit by a car."</p>

<p>Among urban residents of the English-speaking world, the word appears to carry its original connotations (i.e. the facilitation of traffic as a prime purpose, and "street life" as an incidental benefit). For instance, a <i>New York Times</i> writer lets casually slip the observation that automobile-laden <a href="/wiki/Houston_Street" title="Houston Street" class="mw-redirect">Houston Street</a> is "a street that can hardly be called 'street' anymore, transformed years ago into an eight-lane raceway that alternately resembles a Nascar event and a parking lot."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Published in the paper's Metro section, the article evidently presumes an audience with an innate grasp of the modern urban role of the street. To the readers of the Metro section, vehicular traffic does not reinforce, but rather detracts from, the essential "street-ness" of a street.</p>

<p>At least one map has been made to illustrate the geography of naming conventions for thoroughfares; street, avenue, boulevard, circle, and other suffixes are contrasted against one another.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
